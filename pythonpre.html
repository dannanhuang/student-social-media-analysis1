After data preprocessing, we further quantify the linear relationships between the core variables, with specific explanations as follows:

Correlation Analysis

  1. Research Objective: To quantify the linear correlations between average daily mobile phone usage hours and key variables (nightly sleep duration, mental health score, and addiction score).

  2. Analysis Method: Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient is adopted, which is suitable for scenarios where both variables are continuous and approximately normally distributed. It mainly measures the strength and direction of the linear association between variables.

  3. Result Explanation: By outputting the correlation coefficient (r-value) and significance level (p-value), the reliability and degree of association between variables are clarified. The r-value ranges from [-1, 1], and p<0.05 indicates that the association is statistically significant.

Considering the potential deviation of data from normal distribution or the presence of ordinal variables, we further conduct non-parametric correlation analysis to verify the robustness of the results. Specific explanations are as follows:

  1. Research Objective: To verify the robustness of the associations between average daily mobile phone usage hours, nightly sleep duration, mental health score, and addiction score, avoiding biases caused by unmet normal distribution assumptions;

  2. Analysis Method: Spearman's rank correlation coefficient is adopted, a non-parametric statistical method that does not require data to be normally distributed. It is suitable for continuous and ordinal categorical variables, focusing on measuring the strength and direction of the monotonic association between variables;

  3. Result Explanation: The rank correlation coefficient (-value, rho) and significance level (p-value) are output. The -value ranges from [-1, 1], and p<0.05 indicates that the monotonic association is statistically significant. The results can be cross-validated with Pearson correlation analysis.

def scatter_with_fit(x, y, xlabel, ylabel, title):
    plt.figure(figsize=(6,4))
    plt.scatter(x, y)
    m, b = np.polyfit(x, y, 1)
    xs = np.linspace(x.min(), x.max(), 100)
    plt.plot(xs, m*xs + b, color='red')
    plt.title(title)
    plt.xlabel(xlabel)
    plt.ylabel(ylabel)
    plt.show()

Question 5 Threshold Exploration Problem: Is there a "critical point" in usage hours beyond which negative impacts increase?

In[]:
# The list of stored results
results = []
# Iterate from threshold 1 to 9
for threshold in range(1, 10):
# Iterate through the thresholds from 1 to 9 to divide the population into "above the threshold" and "below the threshold" groups.
    above_threshold = df['Usage_Hours'] > threshold
    below_threshold = ~above_threshold  # On the contrary, "not exceeding the threshold"

    # Calculate the difference in academic impact (above average - below average)
    academic_above = df.loc[above_threshold, 'Academic_Impact'].mean()
    academic_below = df.loc[below_threshold, 'Academic_Impact'].mean()
    academic_diff = academic_above - academic_below

    # Calculate the difference in mental health scores (above average - below average)
    mental_above = df.loc[above_threshold, 'Mental_Health_Score'].mean()
    mental_below = df.loc[below_threshold, 'Mental_Health_Score'].mean()
    mental_diff = mental_above - mental_below

    # save the result
    results.append({
        'Threshold': threshold,
        'AcademicImpactDiff': academic_diff,
        'MentalHealthDiff': mental_diff
    })

# transfer to DataFrame
results_df = pd.DataFrame(results)
